01 · Business Problem

The Question That
Prompted This Analysis

"Who are our best customers, who is slipping away, and where should we focus retention spend?"

— Head of Growth, Brazilian E-Commerce Company

A mid-sized Brazilian e-commerce company had two years of transaction data but no structured way to understand customer behaviour. Marketing budget was being spread equally across all customers — including those already churned.

⚠️
No customer visibility. No existing segmentation framework to distinguish high-value loyalists from one-time buyers.
💸
Budget inefficiency. Retention spend applied equally to churned customers, at-risk customers, and active loyalists with no differentiation.
📉
Unknown churn drivers. No data-backed understanding of what causes customers to disengage — whether delivery, category, or behaviour.
🎯
Required output. An actionable segmentation model and churn analysis to direct where re-engagement investment should go first.

03 · Methodology

Five-Phase Analysis
— Click to Expand Each Phase

01
Data Exploration & Quality Check
Validation · Null Check · Join Integrity
▼

Validated row counts, null values, date ranges, order status distribution, and join integrity across all five tables before any analysis began. No critical nulls were found.

⚠️ Exclusions: 775 orders with no line items and 1,604 items with no matching product were identified and excluded from downstream analysis.
02
Customer Behaviour Metrics
Window Functions · LTV · Inter-Purchase Intervals
▼

Built foundational per-customer metrics including total orders, total spend, average order value, first and last purchase dates, and inter-purchase intervals using window functions.

📊 Exclusion note: Review score data covered fewer than 0.3% of customers (270 out of 96,000+) — excluded as statistically unreliable.
03
RFM Segmentation
NTILE(5) · Recency · Frequency · Monetary
▼

Customers were scored on Recency, Frequency, and Monetary dimensions using NTILE(5) window functions, then labelled into six actionable segments.

🔧 Deduplication: Same-day multi-item orders were deduplicated at the date level to avoid inflating frequency scores.
04
Churn Analysis
180-Day Threshold · Delivery Correlation · DATEDIFF
▼

Churn was defined as no purchase in 180 days from the reference date (2018-10-18). Churn rates were measured overall, by product category, and correlated against delivery delay performance.

📦 Delivery delay measured using DATEDIFF between estimated and actual delivery dates across 4 performance tiers.
05
Stakeholder Summary View
vw_customer_summary · Unified Reporting · Reusable View
▼

All dimensions — RFM segment, churn status, and delivery bucket — combined into a single reusable view (vw_customer_summary) for ongoing reporting and stakeholder access.

✅ Single unified view allows non-technical stakeholders to query customer status without running complex multi-phase SQL.

05 · Recommendations

Four Prioritised Actions
— In Order of Urgency

01
Launch targeted re-engagement for At Risk segment
37,344 customers representing 40% of total revenue are slipping away. These customers have purchase history and brand familiarity — re-engagement cost is significantly lower than new customer acquisition. A time-limited discount or personalised product recommendation campaign targeted specifically at this group should be the immediate priority.
02
Nurture Promising segment before they disengage
30,085 recently active customers with no repeat purchase yet. This is the lowest-cost conversion opportunity in the entire base. A follow-up email sequence or loyalty incentive triggered 30–60 days after first purchase could materially increase the repeat purchase rate from this group.
03
Fix logistics before any further marketing investment
6,317 customers received late deliveries and churn at up to 91.4%. Investing in marketing while the delivery experience remains broken is a leaky bucket strategy. Reducing average delay in the 8–14 day and 15+ day buckets must be treated as a prerequisite to retention spend — not a separate operational issue.
04
Build a VIP programme for Champions
Only 882 Champions exist in the customer base, but they spend 2.3× the average customer. This group should be identified, tracked, and treated distinctly — early access, dedicated support, or loyalty rewards. Losing a Champion is disproportionately costly compared to losing an average customer.
